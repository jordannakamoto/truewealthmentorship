Book Excerpt: The Magic Wand

"If we were meeting here 3 years from today, what needs to have happened for you both personally and professionally for you to feel happy with your progress?"

Years ago, Dan Sullivan developed former question as an exercise for financial advisors. For 15 minutes, participants would write their answers un-interrupted without second-gueses or edits to what came to mind. Sullivan got the people to share what they had written to others in the room and then look over their list imagining that everything would come true; everything they wanted would actually happen. Waving a "magic wand" the advisor would ask people how achieving their desires felt to them - usually "happy" or "great" - something very positive.

Now the real magic part of the exercise was when the wand was waved again, but this time nothing on the list were to have happened - and people followed by feeling "sad" or "depressed".

Finally, the Clarity Exercise: the advisor had changed his mind, the wand was to be waved once more but *only* one thing and one thing only would come true over the next three years. What was that thing? The goal or dream that mattered most to us in the next three years, the one that needs to have happened, is what the "magic" highlighted for us to prioritize.

But I was never completely comfortable with that phrase, "needs to have happened", because what needs to happen isn't necessarily within people's awareness or self-knowledge. In other words, we generally don't have the knowledge of what we want or need. As Abraham Maslow says:

"It isn't normal to know what we want. It is a rare and difficult psychological achievement"

We're influenced by our experiences, conditioning, media of all sorts, significant events and people in our lives and even by states of mind, moods, or anything else at the time we might take this exercise. Thus, whenever we say we want something, our responses might be tentative at best: for this reason I've added fulfillment, meaning, and joy to the question; modifying 'needs' and 'success' so we can better approach what we value:

"If we were meeting here 3 years from today, what might have happened - both personally and professionally - for you to feel that you are living a happy, fulfilling, meaningful, and joyful life?"

That was my goal for the material that I teach, that eventually wound up in the form of this book. To get us thinking the right thoughts and taking time the right way to understand strategy like a financial advisor but value like a philosopher. I hope you enjoy reading both the story of my personal career as well as all of the lessons and exercises I have included. - Ron
